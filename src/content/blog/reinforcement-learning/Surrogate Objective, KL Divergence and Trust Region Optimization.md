# 1. Introduction

The objective of Reinforcement Learning is deceptively simple.

Given an environment, an agent should learn a policy that maximizes the cumulative reward obtained through interaction with that environment.

Mathematically, if a policy is parameterized by $\theta$, the objective is written as

$$
J(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}
\left[
R(\tau)
\right],
$$

where

- $\tau$ denotes a trajectory generated by the policy,
- $R(\tau)$ is the total return accumulated along that trajectory.

This equation represents the quantity we ultimately wish to maximize.

Everything in policy optimization—REINFORCE, Actor-Critic methods, TRPO, PPO, and even RLHF—can be viewed as different strategies for maximizing this objective.

At first sight, the optimization problem appears straightforward.

1. Execute the current policy.
2. Measure the return.
3. Improve the policy.
4. Repeat.

Unfortunately, one subtle detail makes the problem considerably more difficult than it first appears.

Every time the policy changes, **the probability distribution over trajectories changes as well**.

This single observation is responsible for almost every algorithmic innovation in modern policy optimization.

---

# 2. The Data Distribution Problem

Suppose we execute a policy$\pi_{\text{old}}$ inside an environment.

After interacting with the environment for some time, we collect a dataset of trajectories

$$
\mathcal{D}
=
\{
(s_1,a_1),
(s_2,a_2),
\ldots,
(s_N,a_N)
\}.
$$

These trajectories are not arbitrary.

Every state visited, every action selected, and every reward received was generated according to the probability distribution induced by the policy $\pi_{\text{old}}.$

Now suppose we perform a gradient update. The policy parameters change,producing a new policy$\pi_\theta.$ Although the parameter update may appear numerically small,it fundamentally changes the probability distribution from which future trajectories will be generated.

The dataset $\mathcal{D}$ no longer represents experience generated by the current policy. Instead, it represents experience generated by a policy that no longer exists.

This immediately creates a dilemma. Should we continue optimizing using stale trajectories, or should we discard the dataset and collect a completely new batch?

---

# 3. The Cost of Fresh Data

From a mathematical perspective, the correct answer is obvious.Every expectation appearing in the policy gradient theorem assumes that samples are drawn from the **current** policy. 

Consequently, after every policy update, we should ideally execute the new policy, collect another batch of trajectories, estimate a new gradient, and repeat the optimization. Unfortunately, this approach is extremely expensive. In many reinforcement learning problems, interacting with the environment dominates the total computational cost.

Examples include

- robotic manipulation,
- autonomous driving simulators,
- molecular simulations,
- financial trading environments,
- and Large Language Models trained with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

Generating trajectories in these environments can require seconds, minutes, or even hours of computation. If each batch of experience could be used for only a single gradient update, the majority of computational effort would be spent collecting data rather than learning from it. 

Consequently, the limiting factor in modern Reinforcement Learning is rarely optimization.
Instead, it is **sample efficiency**—how much learning can be extracted from every trajectory collected. This observation motivates one of the central questions of modern policy optimization.

> **Can we estimate the quality of a new policy without executing it inside the environment?**

If the answer is yes, then the same batch of trajectories could be reused multiple times, dramatically reducing the amount of interaction required with the environment. Fortunately, Importance Sampling provides exactly the mathematical tool required to answer this question.

---

# 4. Reusing Experience Through Importance Sampling

In the previous note, we showed that an expectation under one probability distribution can be rewritten as an expectation under another distribution by introducing an importance ratio.

Applying the same idea to policy optimization,

the probability ratio between two policies becomes

$$
r(\theta)
=
\frac
{\pi_\theta(a|s)}
{\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)}.
$$

This quantity measures how much more—or less—likely the new policy is to choose an action that was originally selected by the old policy.

Suppose that each sampled action is associated with an estimated advantage

$$
A(s,a),
$$

which measures how much better or worse that action performed relative to the average action in the same state.

Importance Sampling tells us that we can estimate the improvement of the new policy using the trajectories collected by the old policy simply by reweighing every sampled action according to the probability ratio.

This leads directly to one of the most important equations in modern Reinforcement Learning,

$$
\boxed{
L(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\pi_{\text{old}}}
\left[
r(\theta)
A(s,a)
\right].
}
$$

This expression is known as the **surrogate objective**. It estimates the expected improvement of the new policy using trajectories generated entirely by the previous policy. Instead of collecting fresh experience after every gradient step, we reuse the existing batch and correct for the difference between the two policies using the importance ratio. 

At first glance, the surrogate objective appears to solve the data efficiency problem completely. It allows multiple policy updates to be performed using a single batch of trajectories, dramatically improving sample efficiency.

However, this solution introduces a new question.

**How reliable is this estimate when the new policy becomes increasingly different from the policy that generated the data?**

---
# 5. Why the Surrogate Objective Is Only a Local Approximation

The surrogate objective represents one of the most elegant ideas in modern Reinforcement Learning. By combining the policy gradient theorem with Importance Sampling, it enables us to estimate the performance of a new policy without interacting with the environment again. If this estimate were exact, policy optimization would become remarkably simple: collect a single batch of trajectories, repeatedly maximize the surrogate objective until convergence, and only then gather another batch of experience.

Unfortunately, this strategy does not work.

The surrogate objective is **not** the true reinforcement learning objective. Rather, it is a local approximation constructed using trajectories generated by the old policy. Its accuracy therefore depends critically on the assumption that the new policy remains sufficiently similar to the policy that generated the data.

To understand why this assumption is necessary, we must examine the two approximations implicit in the surrogate objective.

---

## The First Approximation: Correcting Only the Action Distribution

Recall that the surrogate objective is

$$
L(\theta)
=
\mathbb{E}_{(s,a)\sim\pi_{\text{old}}}
\left[
\frac{\pi_\theta(a|s)}
{\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)}
A^{\pi_{\text{old}}}(s,a)
\right].
$$

The importance ratio

$$
r(\theta)
=
\frac{\pi_\theta(a|s)}
{\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)}
$$

corrects for the fact that the new policy assigns different probabilities to actions than the old policy.

Notice carefully what this ratio accomplishes.

It adjusts **only the probability of selecting actions in states that already appear in the collected dataset**.

The states themselves remain fixed.

The trajectories were generated by

$$
\pi_{\text{old}},
$$

and therefore the expectation is still evaluated over the state distribution induced by the old policy,

$$
d^{\pi_{\text{old}}}(s).
$$

The surrogate objective therefore replaces one component of the probability distribution—the action probabilities—while leaving another component—the state visitation distribution—unchanged.

This distinction is subtle but fundamental.

---

## Policies Change the States They Visit

A policy determines far more than which action is selected in a given state. 

Every action influences the future evolution of the environment. Changing the action probabilities therefore changes the probability of reaching future states. Suppose an autonomous robot initially prefers turning left whenever it reaches an intersection. After training, the new policy strongly prefers turning right.

Although the two policies differ only in the probability assigned to a single action, their long-term behaviour may become completely different. The original policy repeatedly visits one region of the environment. The new policy may never enter that region at all. Instead, it begins exploring an entirely different part of the state space.

Consequently, the distribution of visited states changes,

$$
d^{\pi_{\text{old}}}(s)
\neq
d^{\pi_\theta}(s).
$$

The trajectories collected under the old policy therefore become progressively less representative of the behaviour of the new policy.

Importance Sampling corrects the probability of actions **conditioned on the observed states**, but it does not correct for the fact that the new policy may visit an entirely different collection of states.

This is the first reason why the surrogate objective is only an approximation of the true objective.

---

## The Second Approximation: Variance of the Importance Ratio

The second limitation arises from the importance ratio itself.

Suppose the new policy differs only slightly from the old policy.

Then

$$
\pi_\theta(a|s)
\approx
\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s),
$$

and consequently

$$
r(\theta)
\approx
1.
$$

Every observation contributes almost equally to the estimate, and the variance of the surrogate objective remains small.

Now consider a larger policy update.

Suppose that under the old policy,

$$
\pi_{\text{old}}(a|s)=0.01,
$$

whereas under the new policy,

$$
\pi_\theta(a|s)=0.80.
$$

The importance ratio immediately becomes

$$
r(\theta)=80.
$$

A single sampled action now contributes eighty times more than another observation with ratio one.

Although the estimator remains mathematically valid, it becomes statistically unstable. The optimization process is now driven by a small number of highly weighted samples rather than by the dataset as a whole.

This is precisely the variance explosion encountered in ordinary Importance Sampling.

The surrogate objective therefore inherits the principal weakness of Importance Sampling: it becomes unreliable whenever the proposal distribution and the target distribution drift too far apart.

---

## The Surrogate Objective Is a Local Model

The preceding discussion suggests a useful geometric interpretation.Imagine attempting to approximate a curved surface by placing a tangent plane at one point.Near the point of contact, the plane provides an excellent approximation. As one moves farther away, the discrepancy between the plane and the surface increases. Eventually, the tangent plane bears little resemblance to the original surface.The surrogate objective behaves in exactly the same manner.

It is constructed using trajectories generated by $\pi_{\text{old}}.$ As long as the new policy remains close to the old one, the collected trajectories remain representative, the importance ratios remain close to one, and the surrogate objective accurately predicts changes in the true return.

As the policy moves farther away, both assumptions deteriorate simultaneously. The state distribution changes. The importance ratios become increasingly variable.

Consequently, the surrogate objective ceases to be a reliable estimate of the true objective.

For this reason, the surrogate objective should not be viewed as a global objective function. It is more accurately interpreted as a **local model** of policy improvement, valid only within a neighbourhood surrounding the policy that generated the data.

---

## A Natural Question

The discussion above leads directly to one of the central ideas in policy optimization.

If the surrogate objective is trustworthy only when the new policy remains close to the old policy, then the optimizer should never be allowed to move arbitrarily far in a single update.

This immediately raises two fundamental questions.

1. **How should we measure the difference between two policies?**

2. **How small should a policy update be to ensure that the surrogate objective remains reliable?**

The first question introduces **Kullback–Leibler (KL) Divergence**, a principled measure of the distance between probability distributions.

The second question leads to the idea of a **trust region**, the defining concept behind Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO).

---

# 6. Measuring Policy Difference with KL Divergence

In the previous section, we established that the surrogate objective is not an exact replacement for the true reinforcement learning objective. Rather, it is a local approximation whose reliability depends upon the similarity between the new policy and the policy that generated the data. As long as the two policies remain sufficiently close, the trajectories collected under the old policy continue to provide a faithful estimate of the expected improvement of the new policy. However, as the policy moves farther away, this approximation gradually loses its validity. This naturally leads to the following question:

> **How do we quantify the difference between two policies?**

Since a policy is fundamentally a probability distribution over actions conditioned on the current state, comparing two policies is equivalent to comparing two probability distributions. We therefore require a measure that captures differences in probability distributions rather than merely differences in numerical values.

One might initially consider familiar geometric measures such as the Euclidean distance. Although such measures quantify numerical differences between vectors, they fail to capture the information-theoretic nature of probability distributions. Changing the probability assigned to a highly likely action is generally much more significant than changing the probability of an action that is already extremely unlikely. A meaningful measure of policy difference must therefore account for the probabilistic interpretation of these quantities.

The measure that satisfies this requirement is the **Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence**.

---

# 7. Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence

The Kullback-Leibler divergence measures how different one probability distribution is from another. More precisely, it quantifies the amount of information lost when one probability distribution is used to approximate another. If two policies assign almost identical probabilities to every action, their KL divergence is close to zero. Conversely, if the two policies make substantially different decisions, the KL divergence becomes large.

Given two probability distributions $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$, the KL divergence from $Q$ to $P$ is defined as

$$
D_{KL}(P\|Q)
=
\sum_x
P(x)
\log
\frac{P(x)}
{Q(x)}.
$$

For continuous random variables, the summation is replaced by an integral,

$$
D_{KL}(P\|Q)
=
\int
P(x)
\log
\frac{P(x)}
{Q(x)}
\,dx.
$$

Although this expression may appear intimidating at first, its interpretation is remarkably intuitive. Imagine two policies controlling the same agent. If both policies assign nearly identical probabilities to every possible action, replacing one policy with the other would produce almost no observable change in behaviour. Consequently, the KL divergence is close to zero. On the other hand, if one policy strongly favours actions that the other policy almost never selects, replacing one policy with the other fundamentally alters the agent's behaviour. The KL divergence therefore becomes much larger.

An important property of KL divergence is that it is always non-negative,

$$
D_{KL}(P\|Q)\ge0,
$$

and it equals zero if and only if the two distributions are identical. Unlike Euclidean distance, however, KL divergence is not symmetric. In general,

$$
D_{KL}(P\|Q)
\neq
D_{KL}(Q\|P).
$$

For this reason, it is referred to as a *divergence* rather than a true mathematical distance. Nevertheless, it has become one of the most widely used measures of similarity between probability distributions in statistics, information theory, and machine learning.

---

# 8. Trust Region Optimization

Having established a principled measure of policy difference, we can now return to the surrogate objective. Recall that the surrogate objective provides a reliable estimate of policy improvement only while the new policy remains sufficiently similar to the old one. Once the policy begins to move too far away, the approximation deteriorates because the collected trajectories no longer accurately represent the behaviour of the new policy.

The most natural solution is therefore not to abandon the surrogate objective, but to restrict the optimization process itself. Rather than allowing the optimizer to move arbitrarily far in a single update, we constrain every update to remain within a neighbourhood where the surrogate objective is still trustworthy. This neighbourhood is known as the **trust region**.

The term *trust region* originates from numerical optimization. When optimizing a complicated nonlinear function, one often replaces it with a simpler local approximation. Such approximations are only reliable within a limited neighbourhood surrounding the current solution. The optimizer therefore restricts its search to this neighbourhood, trusting the approximation only within that region. Policy optimization adopts exactly the same philosophy. The surrogate objective is treated as a local model of the true reinforcement learning objective, and optimization is restricted to the region in which this model remains accurate.

To express this mathematically, we constrain the average KL divergence between successive policies,

$$
\mathbb{E}_{s}
\left[
D_{KL}
\left(
\pi_{\text{old}}
\|
\pi_\theta
\right)
\right]
\le
\delta,
$$

where $\delta$ is a small positive constant known as the **trust-region radius**. This constraint ensures that every policy update remains sufficiently close to the policy that generated the trajectories, thereby preserving the validity of the surrogate objective.

---

# 9. Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO)

Combining the surrogate objective with the trust-region constraint leads directly to the optimization problem solved by Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO). Instead of maximizing the surrogate objective without restriction, TRPO searches only among policies that satisfy the KL divergence constraint. Formally, the optimization problem is written as

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\max_\theta
\quad&
L(\theta)
\\
\text{subject to}
\quad&
\mathbb{E}_{s}
\left[
D_{KL}
\left(
\pi_{\text{old}}
\|
\pi_\theta
\right)
\right]
\le
\delta.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

This constrained optimization problem captures the central philosophy of TRPO. The objective encourages the policy to improve according to the surrogate objective, while the constraint prevents the optimizer from moving into regions where that objective is no longer reliable. Rather than taking the largest possible improvement suggested by the surrogate objective, TRPO takes the **largest improvement that can still be trusted**.

An intuitive way to understand this optimization problem is to imagine climbing a mountain using a map that is accurate only within a limited radius around your current location. The map tells you which direction is uphill, but only locally. Venturing too far means relying on increasingly inaccurate information. A sensible strategy is therefore to climb as high as possible while remaining inside the region where the map is known to be reliable. The trust region serves exactly this purpose. It defines the neighbourhood within which the surrogate objective remains a faithful guide for policy improvement.

---

# 10. From TRPO to PPO

Although TRPO provides a principled solution to stable policy optimization, it comes at a significant computational cost. Every policy update requires solving a constrained optimization problem involving second-order information, making the algorithm considerably more complex than conventional gradient descent methods. While TRPO offers strong theoretical guarantees, these guarantees are obtained through sophisticated optimization machinery that is often expensive to implement and computationally demanding to execute.

This naturally motivates the next stage in the evolution of policy optimization algorithms. If the essential idea behind TRPO is simply to prevent the policy from changing too much in a single update, is it possible to achieve the same behaviour without solving a constrained second-order optimization problem?

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) answers this question in the affirmative. Rather than enforcing the trust region explicitly through a KL divergence constraint, PPO incorporates the same principle directly into its objective function by clipping the importance ratio. This seemingly simple modification preserves much of the stability of TRPO while allowing the entire optimization procedure to be performed using ordinary first-order gradient descent.

In this sense, PPO should not be viewed as an entirely new algorithm. It is best understood as a computationally efficient approximation to the trust-region optimization problem solved by TRPO.

---

# Summary

The surrogate objective enables policy improvement to be estimated using trajectories collected under an older policy, dramatically improving sample efficiency through the use of Importance Sampling. However, this objective is fundamentally a local approximation whose reliability depends upon the similarity between successive policies. KL divergence provides a principled measure of this similarity, allowing the optimizer to quantify how much the policy changes after each update.

Trust Region Policy Optimization combines these ideas by maximizing the surrogate objective while explicitly constraining the average KL divergence between successive policies. This ensures that every update remains within the region where the surrogate objective is a reliable approximation of the true reinforcement learning objective. Although solving this constrained optimization problem is computationally expensive, it establishes the conceptual foundation upon which Proximal Policy Optimization is built.

> [!important]
> **The surrogate objective tells us how to improve the policy. KL divergence tells us how far we may safely move. The trust region combines these two ideas, ensuring that every policy update is both ambitious enough to improve performance and conservative enough to remain reliable.**
